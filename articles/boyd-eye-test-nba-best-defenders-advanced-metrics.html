Quantifying the Eye Test

What do the advanced metrics say about the NBA’s best defenders?

Nick Boyd

April 28, 2026

Defense in basketball is notoriously difficult to quantify.

“No one has mastered how to analyze defense,” Daryl Morey, the 76ers’ president of basketball operations, told The Ringer’s Zach Kram in 2021. “It’s very complicated.”

There is no single number that can tell you how good of a defender an individual NBA player is. Plus/minus numbers are too reliant on team’s overall performance and don’t show an individual player’s impact very well. Counting stats like blocks and steals are nice, but they don’t tell the whole story.

Historically, watching game film has been the go-to method for scouts and fans alike to evaluate how good players are defensively. But recently, the NBA and its broadcast partners have invested heavily in on-court tracking, using high-speed HawkEye cameras in each arena to collect extensive player and shot data as teams seek an analytical edge. This avalanche of data has led to new advanced statistics that can measure every aspect of on-court play - including defense.

Measuring defense in the NBA comes down to three main attributes: player guarding, shot contesting and rebounding. Diving deeper into the numbers shows who excels at which roles and can tell us what kind of defender a player is. There’s still no one perfect metric that can definitively tell a player’s overall defensive success, but by looking at different stats collectively, you can get a clearer picture of the league’s best defenders.

So what metrics are the most valuable? It depends on what you’re trying to find out. The majority of players play specific roles on defense and their defensive stats will often reflect what their role is. And the best of the best are the guys who can do everything well.

The visualization below shows where players rate based on a few advanced metrics via Databallr, an analytics site that pulls from official NBA sources:

Stat Cheat Sheet
STOP% - how much a player forces their opponents to turn the ball over. A higher percentage means they are more disruptive on defense.
DIFF% - the percentage change in an opponent’s field goal percentage when a player contests a shot. A higher percentage means they force more missed shots. Negative is good for this stat.
STOP%+DIFF% - combines the previous two metrics to show which players are good at being disruptive and forcing opponents to miss shots.

The player who stands out the most is Victor Wembanyama, the San Antonio Spurs’ young superstar center. The data sets Wembanyama miles apart from his colleagues as a shot contester and when guarding opponents. His 7-foot-4 frame and 8-foot wingspan make him a freak of nature on the court and allow him to affect the game defensively in ways that have never been seen before in the NBA. Wembanyama can defend in ways that other players just can’t.

Proving the numbers aren’t full of it? The 22-year-old Frenchman became the first player to win the NBA Defensive Player of the Year unanimously this season.

"[Wembanyama] is a lot different than a typical defender," teammate Stephon Castle told ESPN. "Most of the time when somebody wins Defensive Player of the Year, it's like more team defense and obviously, you have to be a great defender as well. But I feel like for Vic, he could've won it on any team in the league."

Positional role plays a factor in what certain players are good at too. The data shows that centers and big forwards tend to be better at forcing misses because they are tasked with contesting more shots and use their taller frames and longer arms to force more difficult shots. On the flipside, tenacious guards tend to be better defending on-ball and forcing turnovers, because they can use their quickness and agility to stick with ball-handlers and force mistakes. The average big man doesn’t move quickly enough to be as effective on-ball and the average guard doesn’t have the height or length to be elite at forcing misses.

Diving deeper into the shot-contesting side, there’s a similar correlation with opponent shot difficulty. These measurements use player tracking data to see which players force opponents to take harder shots (dSQ) and how well they affect players making or missing their shots (dContest)

Big men are kings here. They frequently face the most shot attempts per game inside near the rim and the most effective can force offenses to gameplan around avoiding them. Take Rudy Gobert for example. Gobert grades out as the league’s best pure shot-contester, pairing his size with next-level defensive instincts and positioning to block shots, force misses and make opposing players decide to not even bother shooting the ball altogether.

"He definitely makes you work a little bit more for every shot," Nuggets’ star Nikola Jokic told SI’s Logan Struck before their first-round playoff series against Gobert’s Timberwolves. "He's changing your shot in every possible way."

Fellow bigs like Chet Holmgren, Evan Mobley, Isaiah Stewart, and Wembanyama have high-rated tracking data too. Meanwhile, guards like Derrick White and Shai Gilgeous-Alexander also shine here, using their length and IQ to close out and force opponents into missing shots further out in the mid-range and from three. Wembanyama, once again, grades nearly off the chart with the highest dContest score in the league. He is the rare big man who is just as effective contesting jumpshots on the perimeter as he is with shots inside to the rim.

Rebounding is the side of defense that gets the least attention, but it’s one of the most important. Some players, like Jokic, aren’t exceptionally good at guarding other players or forcing misses but they are incredible at positioning and boxing out for defensive rebounds. Consistently flipping the possession arrow to set up the offense after a hard-fought miss is the quiet engine that powers winning teams.

Stat Cheat Sheet
DREB - defensive rebounds per game
DRB% - percentage of team’s total DREB a player is responsible for
dREB impact - a metric that grades how impactful a player’s rebounding skill is

The standouts are mostly big men again - the aforementioned Jokic and Wembanyama, Utah’s Jusuf Nurkic and Miami’s Bam Adebayo. The rebounding impact numbers strip away the rebound totals and look at how players are getting their rebounds. Are they uncontested and easy? Or are they fighting for a box out that prevented an offensive player from getting the ball? Most of the big men who rack up loads of rebounds are near the top in impact metrics. The guards are a different story - some of the guards with the highest rebounding impact don’t have a lot of rebounds, but their rebounding skill is above average for their position and size.

So what matters more: the eye test or the metrics? The best answer is a mix of both. There’s still no great way to measure things like perimeter pressure, defensive effort or how well a defender affects their opponent’s decision-making. The numbers just let us compare players without having to grind hours of tape. While figures still may not be perfect, more often than not, the numbers and the play on film tend to agree.
