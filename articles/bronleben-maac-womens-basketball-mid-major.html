How is MAAC Women’s Basketball Changing and Impacting Mid-major Basketball?

This story goes and looks at the MAAC Women’s Basketball compared to the rest of the NCAA Division I. It explains how Quinnipiac and Fairfield have taken control of the MAAC and how mid-major basketball will continue to change

Brittney Bronleben

April 28, 2026

For a long time, the story of women's basketball in the Metro Atlantic Athletic Conference (MAAC) was shaped by the long-lasting shadow of the Marist dynasty. But as we look at the scene in the spring of 2026, the difference in the conference isn't about one team's decade-long hold anymore. Instead, it's a high-stakes, two-sided rivalry that has brought the league's national profile to its highest point in years. Women’s Division 1 basketball is split into two groups: the big Power Four schools and the mid-major. Mid-major hoops is what keeps college basketball going.

With this change, we see a drop-off from the best teams in the NCAA to some of the worst. All within one conference, can there really be a difference, and the answer is yes. Quinnipiac and Fairfield rank in the top 100 teams, while teams like Niagara rank at the bottom.

The Fairfield dynasty is the biggest change in the current MAAC era. The Stags won their third straight MAAC Championship after beating Quinnipiac 51-44 in March 2026. Under Coach Carly Thibault-DuDonis, who just became the fastest coach in conference history to reach 100 wins. Fairfield didn't just win in 2026; they dominated the stats, leading the league in points per game (75.0) and field goal percentage (45.2%). The way Fairfield plays is very different from the way the "old" MAAC did. Their identity is based on a modern, high-efficiency offense led by stars like Jillian Huerter and Meghan Andersen.

While Fairfield represents the conference's offensive ceiling, the 2025–2026 season also emphasized an intriguing stylistic contrast between the top two teams. Even though Quinnipiac claimed the top tournament seed, they did so by relying on defensive grit. The Bobcats had the league's best defense at season's end, allowing only 52.5 points per game.

Fairfield's fast pace and Quinnipiac's strong defense are at odds, creating a rivalry reminiscent of the best games in the Power 4 conferences. The championship game in Atlantic City, where the two teams fought it out in a low-scoring defensive battle, showed that the MAAC has moved away from "run and gun" basketball and toward a more strategic, high-IQ game.

We can't ignore the "difference" in structure in the MAAC. The addition of Sacred Heart and Merrimack in 2024 is official, and the standings have changed as a result. In only their second year in the conference, Merrimack became a real threat by finishing second in the league in scoring offense. Adding these schools to the bracket hasn't just made it bigger; it's also made it harder to make mistakes. Teams like Iona and Siena, which used to be "safe" wins, aren't anymore. This is clear from the 2026 tournament, where late-round upsets almost knocked out the top seeds. The middle of the pack mostly has similar stats.

Ultimately, the defining difference in MAAC women's basketball today is the evolution of the mid-major experience into something more like a professional environment. With NIL changes and intensified coaching competition, the conference is a destination rather than a launching point. The 2026 season proved a team can no longer simply be "good"—they must excel at something, whether that's the Stags' shooting or the Bobcats' defense. The MAAC’s relentless drive for excellence has earned it a strong reputation among mid-majors heading into the 2026–27 cycle.
