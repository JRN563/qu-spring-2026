The Curse of 35: The UFC’s Most Unforgiving Deadline

Heavyweights can rely on raw power until their 40s. But for fighters 170 lbs and below, their 35th birthday is an eviction notice

Ben Busillo

April 28, 2026

Father time is undefeated.

In sports, this rule is universal law. Age will make a once-as-fast lighting point guard – who relied on his quick first step to get to the rim – an obsolete playmaker fading in the rotation. Age makes a third baseman’s once-elite contact become a rarity due to his fading bat-to-ball skills.

There’s no line in the sand, however, when a player will become “washed up” due to their age. Many players reinvent themselves to delay the inevitable effects of age. MLB pitcher CC Sabathia was a hard fastball pitcher for the Cleveland Indians, and then reinvented himself as a crafty left hander as his old age couldn’t get that velocity up anymore.

George Foreman was quick and explosive in his younger days, but then changed his approach as an always advancing, all-power menace, which helped him recapture the heavyweight championship at 45 and 299 days.

Though Father Time’s arrival varies depending on the individual and sport, he always comes through.

In the UFC, there is no expectation to this rule.

However, unlike other sports, there seems to be a clear line in the sand in lower weight classes when Father Time touches down.

From 2010-2025, in title fights in weight classes from 125 lbs (flyweight) to 170 (welterweight), male fighters aged 35 and older hold an abysmal record of 4-26 against opponents under the age of 35.

It seems as though in a sport as brutal as mixed martial arts, the margin of error is near non-existent, as fighters cannot afford to make even the simplest of mistakes in the cage.

One miss by 38-year-old Paul Goldschmidt on a fast ball might not completely ruin his performance or significantly alter his career, but the same slowed reflexes that caused 36-year-old lightweight contender Charles Oliveira to slip on a haymaker have further consequences in the MMA sphere.

Martial arts in lighter weight classes is unforgivingly dependent on athletic ability.

Lighter fighters possess a dynamic range of motion and a complex, fast-twitch arsenal that heavyweights simply cannot replicate. But there is a cruel irony to this advantage: the diverse, highly athletic toolkit that makes lower weight classes so spectacular is exactly what depletes exponentially once a fighter hits their mid-30s.

There will never be a scenario like we see in heavyweight, where a 6-foot 3-inch tall and obese Derrick Lewis can knock everyone out and fight for a championship belt.

The five “cursed” weight classes (Flyweight, Bantamweight, Featherweight, Lightweight, and Welterweight) hold the five worst win percentages for aged 35 and older fighters in title fights.

Lighter fighters get so much more range of motion and a lot more full arsenal than their heavyweight counterparts. That being said, that diverse well rounded arsenal gets depleted exponentially as you age into your mid-30s.

The previously mentioned Derrick Lewis is 41-years-old, and still a top ten heavyweight in the UFC rankings.

For heavyweights, raw power is the last trait to fade. But for those competing at 170 pounds and below, where victory hinges on microscopic advantages in reaction time, your 35th birthday is an absolute eviction notice.

By visualizing the exact age these win percentages plummet, and comparing those statistics directly against the heavier divisions, the numbers expose the truth.

From 2010 to 2024, out of 3,875 total fights in weight classes from Flyweight to Welterweight, 36-year-olds have a total of 92 wins (0.02%).

In the same time frame, out of 1,724 total fights in weight classes from Middleweight to Heavyweight, 36-year-olds have a total of 90 wins (0.05%).

Even though the lighter weight classes have a sample size 2.25 times larger, 36-year-old fighters in both groups yield virtually the exact same number of victories.

At 29 years old, former featherweight and current lightweight champion Ilia Topuria has become the UFC’s ultimate grim reaper for aging legends. In his three career title bouts, the young Spaniard faced off against Alexander Volkanovski, Max Holloway, and Charles Oliveira.

Volkanovski and Oliveira were 35 years old at the time of their fights and Holloway was 33.

While the older veterans kept the action razor-close and matched Topuria strike-for-strike, the endings were identically brutal, with all three fights ending in a devastating, flatlining knockout by Topuria.

Topuria’s wins are a case study for how the mileage on a fighter’s “chin” (their ability to take strikes to the face) is the make or break for older veterans in lighter weight classes. Max Holloway — who made his UFC debut in 2012 — holds the UFC record for most strikes absorbed in the history of the organization, and even though he was two years beneath the cursed age of 35 when he fought Topuria, the principle remains the same. Sustaining that type of damage does horrible things to your repertoire.

This summer, Topuria is set to face another aging legend of the sport: 38-year-old interim lightweight champion Justin Gaethje at UFC Freedom 250. Gaethje is an excellent boxer, but just like Volkanovski, Holloway, and Oliveira, his ability to take the big shot will ultimately be his downfall.

When the physical tools of a lighter fighter evaporate, they don’t just slowly fade from the fight — they get violently evicted from it. The data on how these 35-and-older fighters lose their title bouts serves as the most brutal proof of this reality.

Lighter fighters possess a dynamic range of motion and a complex, fast-twitch arsenal that heavyweights simply cannot replicate. But that diverse, well-rounded toolkit, and their ability to take hard shots deplete exponentially once a fighter hits their mid-30s.
