Regular Season Success and March Failures: The Era of Quinnipiac Basketball

Despite lots of great wins under Tom Pecora, Quinnipiac’s regular season success hasn’t continued into the postseason

Beckett Calkins

April 28, 2026

Three seasons ago, the Quinnipiac men’s basketball program had experienced very little success since making the jump up to Division-I in 1998. That changed a few years ago, with the hire of Tom Pecora as head coach. With the hire of Pecora came regular season success. For Quinnipiac the regular season success has been impressive but it all comes down to March.

When Quinnipiac first escalated to Division-I, the program joined the NEC and had just two 20+ win seasons in 15 years. In 2013 the Bobcats made the jump to the MAAC under head coach Tom Moore.

Moore lasted just three seasons in the MAAC after having a worse conference record in each of those years. That is when Quinnipiac made the coaching change to Baker Dunleavy. Dunleavy, who came from a basketball family with both his dad and brother spending time in the NBA, was also never able to lead the Bobcats to consistent success on the hardwood. In his six years at the helm the Bobcats never won a conference regular season or tournament title, never had more than 11 conference wins and had only one season (Dunleavy’s last) where they finished with a record over a game over .500.

When Dunleavy resigned after six years at the helm Quinnipiac knew it had to shift its strategy, but the shift wasn’t as big as many around the program thought.

Athletic Director Greg Amodio announced that Pecora would take over as the next Quinnipiac head coach, and while he was on the Bobcats coaching staff already, he wasn’t in an in-game coaching position. His last period as head coach ended at Fordham in 2015 with a dismal 44-106 overall record. Dunleavy took him on as his special assistant in 2017, and by the time the position was ready to be passed on Pecora hadn't been a head coach in eight years but Amodio saw something in the then 65-year-old coach.

Pecora’s first season at the head took Quinnipiac to new heights. A program best 24 wins and 15 conference wins and more importantly its first MAAC regular season title. Once again they sputtered in the postseason, losing in the MAAC semifinals. Year two was equally as successful, with another conference regular season title, and another conference player of the year – but the postseason theme continued. The Bobcats were stunned in the MAAC semifinals again, this time to the Iona Gaels.

This year, despite retaining the MAAC Player of the Year Amarri Monroe, the Bobcats struggled in conference play, going 12-8 and finishing as the four seed, losing in the first round of the MAAC tournament.

Yet despite the postseason failures Quinnipiac’s offense has been the best in the MAAC these last three years, leading the conference in points per game in two of those seasons. The one-year Quinnipiac wasn’t atop the MAAC was 2024-25, when it finished second to Manhattan. While the Bobcats have had extensive offensive success, they haven’t shot the ball very well from the perimeter in any of these seasons. Pecora’s offense is largely transition-based and focuses on Quinnipiac getting out and running fast breaks.

This high-powered offense and star power has translated to the most wins in a 3-year stretch in QU Division-I history. Look at where the program ranks in wins in conference over the last three seasons. With 42 total wins over that timespan, Quinnipiac once again has a lot of regular season success to be proud of. The second closest team is Marist with 37 wins. Teams with far less regular season success in Siena (25 wins), Mount (32 wins) and Iona (32 wins) have won the conference's postseason titles during the Bobcats reign of terror in the regular season.

Why has each of the last three seasons ended in an unfortunate manner? It feels unique every time. Three seasons ago felt like Quinnipiac’s best chance to win the conference. They were the superior team all year long, until they got matched up with a tough, defensive oriented Saint Peter’s team.

There’s a saying in college basketball, “Guards win in March.” In 2025, Quinnipiac got matched up with arguably the best backcourt in the league in Iona. Adam Njie Jr. and Dejour Reeves each put on a show and sent the Bobcats home packing in a relatively easy win.

This year it was almost a mix of the last two seasons. Marist, Quinnipiac's opponent, was known for its defense and was missing a few of its best scorers. That didn’t matter. The Red Foxes shooting guard Justin Menard got hot at the right time once again eliminating QU before the championship game. It’s clear that the success of the season comes down to one week in Atlantic City.
