Seeing a Change: How the NBA 3PT Line Transformed the League

Something so simple, like the 3 point line, is now so crucial to the sport of basketball that we see today

James Kassan

April 28, 2026

Over the last decade, the NBA has undergone one of the biggest offensive transformations in sports, and it can all be traced back to one simple thing: the three- point line.

If you sat down with your dad or even your grandpa and asked about today’s NBA, they’d probably tell you the game looks completely different — and they wouldn’t be wrong. The way teams play offense now compared to even 10–15 years ago has changed drastically. What used to be a secondary scoring option has now become the main focus, and teams are building entire rosters around it.

Not long ago, the league was built around post play and midrange scoring. Big men were the focal point of offenses, and teams relied on getting high-percentage shots near the basket. The three-point shot was definitely part of the game, but it wasn’t something every team prioritized. It was more of a bonus than a strategy.

That started to change in the early 2010s when analytics became a bigger part of how teams operated. Front offices began to realize that the math behind shot selection mattered. A three-pointer, even if made at a slightly lower percentage, is still worth more than a long two. Once teams fully bought into that idea, the shift happened fast.

Offenses started to focus more on spacing the floor, moving the ball, and creating open looks from beyond the arc.

Stephen Curry is a prime example of a three point shooter. His ability to shoot from deep with incredible range and accuracy forced defenses to adjust in ways they never had before. Kids began to idolize him and even yell “Curry!” as they stepped back and shot a three to try and mimic his style of play.

Curry didn’t just make threes—he changed what was considered a good shot. Pulling up from well beyond the line or shooting off the dribble became normal because of him. Along with the dominance of the Golden State Warriors, he helped push the entire league toward a three-point-heavy style.

Because of this, the definition of an ideal NBA player has changed. Shooting is now a must at almost every position. Guards are expected to create and shoot off the dribble, wings need to consistently knock down open shots, and even centers are developing three-point range. The traditional back-to-the-basket big man has become less valuable unless they can adapt.

This shift also led to the rise of the “3-and-D” player — guys who can hit open threes while playing solid defense. These players are extremely valuable because they fit perfectly alongside star players without taking away from the offense. Instead of building around one dominant scorer in the paint, teams now focus on creating space and surrounding their stars with shooters.

Other players have also helped define this era. Klay Thompson has been one of the most efficient high-volume shooters ever, thriving in a system built on movement and spacing. Damian Lillard extended the limits of shooting range, while James Harden made the step-back three a major part of his game.

Roster construction has followed this trend. Teams now prioritize versatility, shooting, and spacing over traditional size and roles. Players who can handle the ball, defend multiple positions, and shoot from deep are in high demand.

Early on, the drop in 3-point percentage among top teams can be tied to the league still being more inside-oriented, where dominant big men and midrange scoring mattered more than spacing. As the game evolved—especially during and after the rise of teams like the Golden State Warriors—the 3-point shot became a much bigger strategic focus. While this evolution has led to more efficient offenses and higher scoring, it has also sparked debate. Some believe the game relies too much on the three-point shot, while others see it as a natural progression.

Either way, there’s no denying it—the three-point shot has changed the way the NBA works. We’re seeing a drastic shift in the amount of shots in a game, and seeing teams evolve even by roster building to adapt to this change.
