How the NHL Has Turned Into a Hotspot for Offense

Scoring is up across the league, and this new era of speed is redefining how teams win

Michael Mackey

April 28, 2026

Over the past two decades, the NHL has undergone a significant transformation, one that is reshaping how offense is created and who is responsible for it. While forwards have traditionally driven scoring, today’s game tells a different story. Defensemen are no longer limited to shutting down opponents and moving the puck out of their own zone. Instead, they are becoming some of the league’s most dynamic and productive point generators, playing a central role in an era defined by rising offense.

Looking at this chart, it is evident that defenseman have become much more involved in the offense of their teams. Defenseman logged a whopping 5,530 points at their peak in 2021-2022, and just last season logged 5,260 points. This is way up from the beginning of the decade, where in 2001-2002, they only logged 3,920 points, and got as low as 2,550 points in the 2012-2013 season. That is over a 100% increase in production. This is largely due to players like Cale Makar, who had 72 points this season, and Evan Bouchard, who had 79 points this season. As the game has evolved, the presence of defenseman getting involved in odd-man rushes, and finding themselves below the circle in the zone has become much greater, leading to more offensive production.

At the same time, overall scoring across the league has steadily increased since the early 2000s. Following the so-called “dead puck era,” where defensive systems and lower-scoring games dominated and stylistic shifts have opened the ice. The result is a faster, more fluid game that prioritizes speed, and offensive creativity.

As seen on this graph, teams are generating more goals per game than they did two decades ago, as the league averaged a massive 3.14 goals a game for each team in 2022-2023 at its peak, and 3.06 goals per team per game just last season. This number is way up from the beginning of the decade, where in 2003-2004, teams averaged just 2.57 goals per team per game. This is largely due to the evolution of the game, as it is much faster today than it was 20 years ago. Players like Connor McDavid and Nathan Mackinnon have some of the biggest impact on this shift.

This shift is not just stylistic, it is strategic. Teams increasingly rely on their defensemen to drive play, stretch opposing defenses, and create mismatches. A defenseman who can produce 60 or more points is no longer a luxury; it is often a necessity for teams with championship aspirations. As a result, organizations are prioritizing mobility, vision, and offensive instincts when developing and acquiring players for the position.

Beyond positional changes, there is also a clear relationship between individual scoring success and team performance. Teams that feature players near the top of the league’s scoring leaderboard consistently find themselves higher in the standings.

Looking at the top of this chart, it is consistent that the players at the top are on Stanley Cup contending teams. Nikita Kucherov of the Tampa Bay Lightning with 119 points, his team is third in the east. Connor McDavid of the Edmonton Oilers with 116 points, his team is second in the Pacific division, and coming off of 2 Stanley Cup Final appearances. The only outlier here is Macklin Celebrini of the San Jose Sharks, who is in fifth place with 96 points. His team is out of the playoffs, but made a significant jump from the season before where his team went from dead last in the league, to just missing the playoffs this year. Elite point producers do more than just fill the stat sheet, they tilt the ice, generate sustained offensive pressure, and force opponents to adjust their defensive strategies. This impact often translates directly into wins over the course of a season.

Taken together, these trends highlight a league that is evolving in both pace and philosophy. Scoring is up, roles are expanding, and defensemen are emerging as key offensive drivers rather than secondary contributors. In this new landscape, success belongs to teams that embrace a more dynamic approach to offense.
