What Does the Hobey Baker Award TRULY Mean?

Breaking down the success of each winner of the "Hockey Heisman" the year they won the award and their success after college

Andrew Reynolds

April 28, 2026

Throughout the 2025-26 college hockey season, the buzz at Quinnipiac University and the M&T Bank Arena centered on one name: Ethan Wyttenbach.

The Quinnipiac freshman and Calgary Flames fifth-round draft pick last year has taken the NCAA by storm. He led the nation in points and put the “Hockey Heisman”, a.k.a. the Hobey Baker Award, within reach for a program that has never claimed it.

Unfortunately for the Long Island native, he fell short of the title, but was awarded the nation's rookie of the year, the second in Quinnipiac program history. The first year has been widely considered the steal of this past draft and has seen a ton of media coverage as the next big name in Calgary and the NHL after Johnny Gaudreau tragically passed away in August 2024.

But as Wyttenbach chases history and his pro dreams, a larger question looms over the trophy– Does winning the Hobey Baker signal the arrival of an NHL superstar, or is it simply the crowning achievement of a collegiate peak?

In the early 1980s and 90s, the award was synonymous with eye-popping offensive totals. Tony Hrkac’s 116-point season in 1987 and Bill Watson’s 109-point campaign in 1985 represented an era that seemed “easy” by today's standards, and are just impossible to reach in today’s modern game.

Take Wyttenbach's nation-leading 59 points on the season. The talent and goaltending have certainly evolved since then, as it is hard even to reach 100 points in a career in this modern era. However, as the game evolved, so did the voters’ definition of “best.”

The data shows a distinct trend toward rewarding defensive impact and efficiency over raw volume. Modern winners like Cale Makar (2019) and Scott Perunovich (2020) secured the trophy with 49 and 40 points, respectively, proving that a defenseman’s ability to control the game is now valued as highly as a forward’s goal-scoring prowess. As mentioned, history shows that points alone don’t guarantee hardware or professional longevity.

Looking at the Hobey winners' scoring line for the year they won, the data shows two things. The first is how little scoring there is anymore compared to 40 years ago, and the selection of the winner relies on the three main scoring lines: goals, assists, and points.

Since its inception, only three players (excluding goalies) have finished outside the top ten in points in the national rankings.

A unique tension in college hockey is the correlation between individual awards and team success. Unlike the NBA MVP, which almost exclusively goes to a top-three seed, the Hobey Baker has a more complicated relationship with the NCAA Tournament.

Across the 8-team, 12-team, and 16-team eras, the characterization of a winner has fluctuated. Does the award go to the best player on a championship squad, or a lone star on a team that didn’t qualify for the NCAA tournament? Historically, the Frozen Four appearance has been a strong indicator of a winner’s profile.

While skaters dominate the conversation, the “goalie outliers” provide a different data set. Only three netminders have won the award in over 40 years: Robb Stauber (1988), Ryan Miller (2001), and Dryden McKay (2022). Considering there is already an award for the nation’s top netminder, the fact that these guys were named the top players in the NCAA, not a player, shows how dominant their performances were. Ryan Miller is the standout out of this group, playing 796 NHL career games, while the other two have not even reached 100 combined.

This disparity raises the ultimate question for any Hobey winner: Does the award signal the coming of an instant NHL superstar or an athlete who peaked at the collegiate level? Ultimately, the Hobey Baker is a snapshot of excellence, and not necessarily a crystal ball. For the Jack Eichels and Cale Makars of the world who transition seamlessly to NHL stardom, there are winners whose games were perfectly suited for the college rink but struggled with the speed and size of the pro level, like an Andy Miele or a Scott Perunovich.

When it comes to Ethan Wyttenbach, he will return to Quinnipiac for another year of development before presumably making the jump to pro in the next couple of years. In doing so, he enters the rare territory of a returning nation-leading scorer with a target on his back. If he can elevate his game beyond the historic benchmarks of his first season, Wyttenbach won't just be preparing for the Calgary Flames; he’ll be cementing his status as one of, if not the greatest player in program history, and potentially joining the "Rising Stars" and "NHL Icons" level of the chart above if he is to take home the recognition of the best player in college hockey.
