Who Was the Most Effective Player in the MAAC During the 2025-26 Season?

Determining which player in the conference had the best statistical season in all aspects of the game by compiling advanced analytics

Ben Rickevicius

April 28, 2026

Evaluating talent in college basketball has changed over the past decade with the addition of tracking advanced player metrics. These metrics give programs more information on how players perform in categories outside your typical postgame box score.

When first looking at individual player stats in a specific conference, the first statistic that pops up is always Points per Game (PPG). But the main issue with solely looking at this statistic when evaluating how talented a player is that the stat only measures scoring, and not other impactful elements like rebounding, playing defense or playmaking.

One of the most exciting mid-major conferences in men’s basketball is the Metro Atlantic Athletic Conference (MAAC). The conference has produced champions that cause mayhem in March, like the 2022 Saint Peter’s team that upset Purdue in the Round of 64.

The MAAC Player of the Year award went to freshman point guard Kevair Kennedy, who averaged 18.4 PPG for Merrimack this season. The Warriors were defeated in the championship game by Gavin Doty and the Siena Saints, who went on to play a tight game against No. 1 seeded Duke in March Madness. Evaluating mid-major talent is something that Power 5 programs will have to continue to perfect, with the transfer portal being one of the best ways to recruit new talent to rosters. Taking into consideration all aspects of the game, was Kennedy truly the best player in the conference this past season?

In order to see which players perform the best on the offensive end of the game, the production that a player puts up must be measured against how many opportunities they get on the court. The graphic below measures every player in the conference’s true shooting percentage against their usage percentage. True shooting percentage looks at how many shots a player is making while weighing the difficulty of them. For instance, three-pointers carry more weight than mid-range shots. The size of the dots representing each player accounts for how many points they scored over the course of the season.

Some of the strongest performers are Kevair Kennedy, Sacred Heart’s Anquan Hill and Dashon Gittens and Quinnipiac’s Amarri Monroe. All four players were at the top of the MAAC in PPG, but something interesting to note is none of these three players are known for their three-point shot. The coaching staff at each school understand how to get the most out of their stars, drawing up more inside looks to get the most out of their skillsets. These four standouts also were the number one options on their respective teams, meaning their usage percentage is towards the higher end of the scale.

Something else that stands out is Iona’s Luke Jungers, who has a very high true shooting percentage, has scored a slightly above average amount of points, but wasn’t on the court as much as some of the frontrunners. Jungers shot 43.9% from three, which helps his true shooting percentage turn out higher. Given more opportunities in the future, Jungers could be a candidate to be one of the best players in the conference on the offensive end next season.

Moving over to the defensive side, things get a little more complicated. Players can impact the game in statistical categories like blocks and steals, but a large part of the defensive end is being able to force missed shots and turnovers for others. Because of the impact that all five players on the court play on defense, it’s important to consider how well a team is playing defensively compared to an individual's performance. Adjusted team defense is measured against individual Bayesian performance rating. Bayesian performance rating takes a look at how many points per 100 possessions better a player’s team is projected to be than its opponent if that player were on the court with nine “average” D1 players.

The highlights include Merrimack's KC Ugwuakazi, who won the MAAC Defensive Player of the year, Marist’s Jaden Daughtry and Rhyon Blackwell, and Iona’s Alliou Fall. Something interesting to note is that Quinnipiac’s Monroe is also towards the elite end of the spectrum on the defensive graph, in addition to his high offensive placement.

Kennedy performs well in many of the offensive sections, but lacks heavily behind the others in defense. While Merrimack had one of the best defenses in the conference this season, there were moments this season when he was attacked defensively, especially in the MAAC championship game. The top performer in the combined categories was Quinnipiac’s Monroe. While many players had better offensive scoring numbers than him, his high defensive Bayesian ranking and +/- separates his performance from the rest of the top 15 players. Some other interesting things to note are Justin Menard’s incredibly high +/- which helps him climb higher than most players with his defensive numbers were placed.

While having access to this information can help MAAC teams bring in talent from other schools, it also hurts them. Many of the top performers from this past season have transferred to a Power 5 conference already or remain in the portal. Kennedy left the Warriors for Wake Forest, Doty left Siena for Syracuse, and Monroe currently sits in the portal waiting to see if he’ll receive another year of eligibility. Some players will stay in the MAAC for another year. For coaching staff, looking at the analytics can help determine which players they should prioritize trying to retain. Prior to Kennedy this season, the last three MAAC Player of the Year winners had been on the team they won the award with for at least a season prior. For fans, it means that the next superstar in the league could already be a part of the conference.
