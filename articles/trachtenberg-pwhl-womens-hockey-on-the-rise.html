The PWHL’s Impact Beyond the Ice

How the start of the PWHL has impacted the growth of women’s hockey for both the players and fans

Brianna Trachtenberg

April 28, 2026

For years, female hockey players' careers ended at the collegiate level. There was no professional league for these athletes to compete in.

There was an attempt to have a professional women’s hockey league with the NHWL/PHF, but not all teams were fully NHL backed and the league had trouble gaining support. While the Women’s National Basketball Association (WNBA) had been founded in 1996 and was backed fully by the NBA, it took the NHL 27 years to do the same.

The Professional Women’s Hockey League (PWHL) was announced by the Mark Walter group in August of 2023, and was backed by the NHL. The inaugural season included six teams located in Boston, Minneapolis-St. Paul, Montreal, New York City, Ottawa and Toronto. The league has since grown to eight teams, with two additions now located in Seattle and Vancouver.

Since the first season, women’s hockey has taken off. Putting the sport on the national level has elevated the level of the game, the fans and the players. They surpassed historic milestones when they achieved over a million fans in attendance after a Detroit Takeover Tour game on March 16, 2025 where there were 14,288 fans in attendance (PWHL). The growth since their first game on Jan. 1, 2024 in Toronto with 2,537 fans has been tremendous.

The growth has gone beyond the national level as well. For the first time ever, young female athletes have someone they can look up to as a role model on the professional level.

The stands have been filled with young fans at every game, cheering on their favorite teams while dressed in team merchandise. It has become a common sight for young girls to hold signs of support throughout the games. The league has celebrated this growth and has embraced the role model position for young athletes everywhere.

Putting women’s hockey on the national level for the first time has also brought the encouragement for girls to go out and join a youth hockey team. The 2024-25 season had the highest amount of female participation in women’s hockey.

Since 2023, the female youth participation in eight and under has grown by over 2,000 participants. In 2023, there were 25,314 participants in eight and under youth hockey. In 2024, there were 27,504. Overall, women’s hockey participation has grown by over 47% in the last two years. For the 2024-25 season, there were a total of 196,788 female participants a part of USA Hockey, ranging from six and under to professional women athletes.

While many may not have thought about women’s hockey before the PWHL, the league has put the game on the map.

During the 2026 Winter Olympics, the USA Women’s hockey team took to the ice and proved why they deserved to be on the international stage.

They earned their third gold medal in the win against Canada during these past games, bringing their overall medal count to eight. The women have earned just as many gold medals as the men’s team, despite women's hockey not being added to the games until 1998, 74 years after the first Winter Olympics.

The success at the Olympics has continued to draw even more fans to the PWHL as the 2026 season returns to play. Post Olympics, the league announced that the finals will be nationally televised for the first time in the United States this season. The milestone marks how much the league has grown in just three years.
