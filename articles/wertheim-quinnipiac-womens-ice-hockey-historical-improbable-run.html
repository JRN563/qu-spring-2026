How Two Historical Seasons Led to One Improbable Run

One of the season's biggest stories in Quinnipiac's women's ice hockey involves an unstoppable junior

Will Wertheim

April 28, 2026

The Quinnipiac Women’s Ice Hockey team’s 2024-25 season ended, once again, in heartbreaking fashion, as they were the last team to be eliminated from NCAA national tournament contention by just fractions of a percent in back-to-back years. Even worse, the future seemed to be bleak. The team had eight seniors departing. Four of those seniors ended up becoming PWHL draft picks, including #6 overall pick Kendall Cooper and star goaltender Kayle Doyle. Two additional players also departed in the transfer portal.

With 11 incoming freshmen, the team wasn’t even ranked in the top three in their conference for the 2025-26 campaign, and the season was widely seen as a “rebuilding year.” What no one expected was the team to go on and have one of their best seasons in program history, capped off with an ECAC championship, their first in ten years and second ever. How did this happen? Was this just another Cinderella story? Did one player carry the team? How did they pull this off?

One of the season’s biggest stories involved junior forward Kahlen Lamarche, who was nearly unstoppable. In 41 games, she tallied 42 goals and 22 assists. If more than a goal per game wasn’t good enough, she also broke several program records, including most goals in a weekend (7), and both the single-season goal and point records. She earned All-ECAC first team, All-American second team, and was a top ten Patty Kazmaier award finalist. Even with her impact, hockey is not an individual sport; there are 11 other forwards, 6 defenders, and a goalie out there every game. So just how impactful was Lamarche compared to her team? Also, how did the last 10 top scorers for the Bobcats compare to their squads?

In the last 10 seasons, Lamarche stands out the most when it comes to a player having the highest percentage of a team’s total points throughout a season. Additionally, she had the highest gap/separation from the next closest teammate in both goals and points in the last 10 years. What is important to note is that in those 10 years, only three top-scoring players have had a “points-gap” in the double digits (Lamarche 2026, Mobley 2023, Cianfarano 2016). Those three years are also the most successful seasons in the program’s history. Sometimes, you need a top scorer to help take your team to the next level, and Lamarche did just that this season.

Kahlen wasn’t the only Quinnipiac player breaking records this season, however. Sophomore goaltender Felicia Frank had a historical season for the Bobcats. This season, she led the NCAA in wins (29) and was second in shutouts (10). She had a 1.56 GAA and a .942 save%. She racked up over 1,000 saves in a single season, giving her the program’s single-season save record. Frank was named to the All-ECAC First Team, announced as the ECAC goalie of the year, and was a top-three finalist for NCAA goalie of the year. With this incredible season, where does she compare to the top 20 goaltenders league-wide for the last 5 years?

While she sits in the top tier of NCAA goalies, there is one thing that also needs to be considered. This season, Frank played in every single game. That’s 2,458 minutes, 207 minutes (roughly three games) more than UConn netminder Tia Chan, who’s second in most minutes played. She is the first goaltender in Quinnipiac history to play in every single game during the season. In the last 10 years, the only goalies to come anywhere near her were 6th-year grad student Logan Angers in 2023/24, Senior Abby Ives in 2019/20, and Senior Sydney Rossman in 2016/17. Reminder: Frank was a sophomore, playing her first year as a starting goalie in college.

A goaltender playing every single game for a full season is almost unheard of in the sport. For her to have played consistently well for as many games as she played is nothing short of an anomaly and is clearly a huge reason why this team had so much success this season.

The main reason for the team’s success came at the hands of two historical seasons. With arguably their best forward as well as best goaltender in program history on the team at the same time, of course, there’s going to be success. But there’s more to this than is involved for it to actually work. There was a collective buy-in from the team. Everyone knew their strengths and weaknesses as a collective and came up with a plan to make the year as successful as possible.

With a full team effort, led by Lamarche and Frank, the team became ECAC champions for just the second time in program history and the first in 10 years. Their season did come to an end short of a National Championship, however, with a loss to Wisconsin, one game away from the Frozen Four. There’s no doubt this team has talent, but if they want to compete with the Wisconsin’s and Ohio States of the women’s college hockey world, they simply need more depth. Scoring needs to come from all four lines, not just one, and they need a backup goaltender who can allow Frank to rest 7-8 games a year. That being said, with both Frank and Lamarche returning next fall, and a stacked freshman class coming in, the team might just have exactly what they need to run back this historical season and make another big step forward.
