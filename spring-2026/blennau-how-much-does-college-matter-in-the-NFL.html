How Much Does College Matter in the NFL?

Power conference teams made up 10 of the 12 teams in this year's College Football Playoffs, but how much does it really mean to play for one?

By James Blennau

April 21, 2026

Power conference teams are everywhere in football. They made up 10 of the 12 teams in this year's College Football Playoff, 22 of 25 in the season's final AP Poll and 28 of 46 bowl game winners. Major networks head to a different powerhouse campus every week for pregame shows, while fans endlessly debate which is better — the SEC? Or the Big Ten?

What does it all matter? How much does it really mean to attend a power-conference program? Do the SEC, Big Ten, Big 12, ACC, and formerly Pac-12 produce better players than minor conferences? Within that, are the SEC and Big Ten truly leaps and bounds ahead of the competition?

The Draft

NFL draftees in earlier rounds tend to be the most instant-impact players from last year's season.

With franchise quarterbacks like CJ Stroud, dangerous edge rushers like Aidan Hutchinson, and versatile playmakers like Jaxon Smith-Njigba all selected within the first 32 picks, their success from contending college teams transferred well to the NFL.

As the Big Ten and SEC continue to consolidate dominance across the football landscape through realignment, their reputations with scouts and coaches only improve as competition intensifies.

While the Big 12 and ACC made a showing in the 2025 draft, it's clear that realignment has caused a major tilt in football's landscape toward its two most dominant conferences.

The Super Bowl

First-round draftees may make a more immediate impact on a team, but winning comes from a full 53-player roster. Star players have always tended to come from more historic programs in more dominant conferences, but what about the rest of the team?

In the past two Super Bowls, it's become clear that players are more likely to come from Big Ten and SEC programs. While blips of stars can exist within teams like Patrick Mahomes' college career at Big 12 program Texas Tech, they appear far less often as the Power Four begins shifting to a "Power Two."

Despite this, non-power conference programs still produce a fair share of championship-winning players. Whether it's stars like Eastern Washington's Cooper Kupp or role players like Toledo's Kareem Hunt, mid-major and non-FBS alumni have made strong impacts on championship rosters for years.

The Hall of Fame

The pinnacle of the sport. It's not just a building in Canton, Ohio, but an achievement to immortalize a gridiron career. So long after a three-to-five-year span in the college ranks, how much do those Saturdays matter in a career that's built on Sundays?

With selections limited only to the best of the best, it appears that it matters more who the player is than what school and conference he played for.

There’s Jared Allen, a physical and dominant defensive end, who finished his career in the top 15 all-time in sacks, and attended a program nowhere near as historic or affluent as many power conference teams are. Then there’s Antonio Gates, widely considered one of the greatest tight ends of all time, who attended minor but proud Kent State. He launched a 16-year career, earning six AP All-Pro selections.

While Tony Boselli at USC and DeMarcus Ware at Auburn represent an ever-strong power-conference contingent, no area or mid-major conference is excluded from Canton simply for a lack of history.

Recent trends show players favoring more affluent and successful programs, and as the rich get richer, players from Group of Six conferences, and even outside the SEC and Big Ten, will find it increasingly difficult to stand out.

It will never be impossible to make a successful NFL career from a program like Boise State or Memphis, but the immediate impact of power-conference players cannot be understated.

However, while power conference teams keep a stranglehold as the premier development programs, it's up to players to decide where is best for themselves. Hall of Famers can come from anywhere, as can any player looking for a successful NFL career.
